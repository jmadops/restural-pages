Neuroplasticity After Stroke

The adult brain retains a capacity for self-repair through neuroplasticity—forming new neural connections. After a stroke, surviving neurons can sprout new axons and assume lost functions.[3][4] But neuroplasticity requires molecular signals. Without them, repair stalls.

Nerve Growth Factor: The Repair Signal

NGF stimulates axonal sprouting, synaptic strengthening, and neuroprotection. After a stroke, NGF spikes briefly then drops. Sustaining elevated NGF gives the brain more capacity to rewire.[5-9]

Lion's Mane: A Natural NGF Stimulator

Hericium erinaceus contains hericenones and erinacines—compounds that cross the blood-brain barrier and stimulate NGF production directly in the central nervous system.[10-11]
